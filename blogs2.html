Identity Theft Facts You Should Be Aware Of

Identity theft complaints increased 40 percent from 2018 to 2019, according to the Consumer Sentinel Network, maintained by the Federal Trade Commission.

There was a victim of identity theft every 2 seconds in 2017. One reason? How easy it is for all of us to share personal information online. How many online accounts do you have? Is it time to close those accounts you no longer need or use?

With this guide, we look at a few identity theft facts you might not know. With these facts, you can learn how to properly secure your data so no one can get a hold of your identity.

Now, are you ready to dive in? Here’s an in-depth look at a few identity theft truths:

How criminals are stealing your information

1.Opening credit-monitoring accounts with Experian, Equifax and other services in victims’ names.

2.Using Google Maps, Zillow, Redfin and other location and real estate services to find locate upscale neighborhoods.

These neighborhoods would be in the range of $700,000 or more, says Nichols. The thieves physically canvass these neighborhoods looking for mail contains personal information that they can swipe. “One multimillionaire client had his details stolen and credit card charges rung up to the tune of over $100,000 as a result,” Nichols says. “You’d be amazed how much personal data is being delivered right to your doorstep.”

3. Public WiFi is an Identity Theif’s Greatest Resource

Public WiFi isn’t the best way to gain internet access. With the correct tools, identity thrives can see what you search, post, and upload to online sites even if it’s password protected. In fact, not only can they monitor your online activity, but they can also see your passwords as you log into sites like your bank account or credit card account.

This can leave you vulnerable to financial identity theft. If they have the right information, they could take over your bank account and steal your money before your able to freeze your account.

It’s important you use caution when utilizing public WiFi. Only go on sites that don’t use passwords that are linked to your bank account, personal email, or other crucial sites.

Some Tips on Identity Theft to Protect Yourself Today

Scam artists "phish" for victims by pretending to be banks, stores or government agencies. They do this over the phone, in e-mails and in the regular mail. Do not respond to any request to verify your account number or password. Legitimate companies do not request this kind of information in this way. Bottom line: Never give out your personal information – unless you made the contact.

Open your credit card bills and bank statements right away. Check carefully for any unauthorized charges or withdrawals and report them immediately. Call if bills don’t arrive on time. It may mean that someone has changed contact information to hide fraudulent charges.

Identity thieves love passwords because they open doors to our personal information. Get tough and organized now. Use different passwords for all your accounts. Make those passwords strong with at least eight characters, including a mix of letters, numbers, and symbols ($+r0^gh@h@). Hide them safely, and keep them handy. Good password practices are work, but fixing an identity theft problem is hard labor!
